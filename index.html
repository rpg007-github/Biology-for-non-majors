01Science & the Scientific Method

Science is a systematic way of learning about the natural world through observation and experimentation. The scientific method is the process scientists use to investigate questions.

The process begins with an observation that sparks a question. A hypothesis is a testable, falsifiable prediction (often "if...then"). An experiment tests the hypothesis under controlled conditions. Data are collected and analyzed, and a conclusion is drawn. The hypothesis is either supported or rejected, never "proven."

TermDefinition
HypothesisTestable, falsifiable prediction
TheoryWell-tested explanation supported by extensive evidence (e.g., evolution, cell theory)
LawStatement describing a consistent pattern in nature (e.g., gravity)
Independent variableWhat the experimenter changes
Dependent variableWhat is measured in response
Control groupNo treatment; provides baseline for comparison
A theory is not "just a guess." In science, a theory is the highest level of confidence: a broad explanation supported by extensive, repeated evidence.

02Chemistry of Life

All living things are made of atoms organized into molecules. Understanding basic chemistry helps explain how cells function, how food provides energy, and how DNA stores information.

Atoms & Bonds

Atoms have protons (+) and neutrons (0) in the nucleus, with electrons (-) orbiting outside. Covalent bonds share electrons (common in biological molecules). Ionic bonds transfer electrons (like NaCl). Hydrogen bonds are weak but critically important in biology, holding water molecules together and stabilizing DNA's double helix.

Water

Water is polar (unequal charge distribution), making it an excellent solvent for polar and ionic substances. Water's key properties: high specific heat (resists temperature change, stabilizes climate and body temp), cohesion (water sticks to water, surface tension), adhesion (water sticks to other surfaces, helps plants pull water up), and ice floats (less dense than liquid water, insulating aquatic ecosystems).

The Four Macromolecules

MacromoleculeBuilding BlockFunctionExamples
CarbohydratesMonosaccharidesQuick energy, structureGlucose, starch, cellulose
LipidsFatty acids + glycerolLong-term energy, membranes, insulationFats, oils, phospholipids
ProteinsAmino acidsEnzymes, structure, transport, defenseHemoglobin, antibodies, collagen
Nucleic acidsNucleotidesGenetic informationDNA, RNA
Building and Breaking
Dehydration synthesis joins monomers by removing water. Hydrolysis breaks polymers by adding water.

Enzymes

Enzymes are proteins that speed up reactions by lowering activation energy. Each is specific to its substrate (lock-and-key model). Enzymes are reusable and not consumed. Extreme temperature or pH can denature an enzyme, destroying its shape and function.

04Energy: Photosynthesis & Respiration

Energy enters ecosystems through photosynthesis and is released for cell use through cellular respiration. These processes are essentially the reverse of each other.

Photosynthesis
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + sunlight → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Occurs in chloroplasts. Captures light energy and stores it as glucose. O₂ is released.
Cellular Respiration
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP

Occurs in mitochondria. Breaks glucose to release ~36-38 ATP. Three stages: glycolysis (cytoplasm, 2 ATP), Krebs cycle (matrix, 2 ATP), ETC (inner membrane, ~34 ATP).

Fermentation

Without O₂, cells use fermentation (only 2 ATP). Alcoholic fermentation (yeast): ethanol + CO₂. Lactic acid fermentation (muscles): lactic acid.

Key Relationship
Photosynthesis stores energy; respiration releases it. The products of one are the reactants of the other. Together they cycle carbon and oxygen through ecosystems.

05DNA, Genes & Protein Synthesis

DNA stores genetic information in all living things. It determines what proteins a cell makes, and proteins do most of the work.

DNA Structure

DNA is a double helix of nucleotides (phosphate + deoxyribose sugar + base). Bases pair by complementary rules: A-T, G-C. In RNA, uracil (U) replaces thymine: A-U, G-C.

Base Pairing
A-T, G-C. "Apples in the Tree, Cars in the Garage."

Central Dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein

StepProcessWhereWhat Happens
1ReplicationNucleusDNA copies itself before cell division
2TranscriptionNucleusDNA template → mRNA copy
3TranslationRibosomemRNA read → amino acids assembled into protein

A gene is a DNA section coding for a protein. A codon is a 3-base mRNA sequence coding for one amino acid. Start codon: AUG. Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA. Mutations are DNA sequence changes that may alter the protein.

06Cell Division & Reproduction

Mitosis

Produces 2 genetically identical diploid (2n) daughter cells. Used for growth and repair. Phases: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase (PMAT), then cytokinesis.

Meiosis

Produces 4 genetically unique haploid (n) cells (gametes). Two rounds of division. Occurs only in reproductive organs.

FeatureMitosisMeiosis
PurposeGrowth, repairGamete production
Divisions12
Daughter cells2, identical, diploid4, unique, haploid
Genetic variationNoneYes
Genetic variation in meiosis comes from crossing over (prophase I), independent assortment (metaphase I), and random fertilization.

07Genetics & Heredity

Genetics is the study of how traits are passed from parents to offspring. Gregor Mendel discovered the basic rules using pea plants.

Key Vocabulary

TermDefinition
GeneSegment of DNA coding for a trait
AlleleDifferent versions of a gene (e.g., T for tall, t for short)
DominantAllele expressed when one or two copies present (T)
RecessiveAllele expressed only when two copies present (tt)
HomozygousTwo identical alleles (TT or tt)
HeterozygousTwo different alleles (Tt)
GenotypeThe allele combination (TT, Tt, tt)
PhenotypeThe observable trait (tall, short)

Punnett Squares

A Punnett square predicts the probability of offspring genotypes and phenotypes. A monohybrid cross (Tt x Tt) gives a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (3 dominant : 1 recessive) and a 1:2:1 genotypic ratio.

Beyond Simple Dominance

PatternWhat HappensExample
Incomplete dominanceHeterozygote is a blendRed x White = Pink
CodominanceBoth alleles fully expressedAB blood type
Sex-linked traitsGene on X chromosomeColor blindness, hemophilia
Polygenic traitsMultiple genes affect one traitSkin color, height
Blood type is a classic genetics example. A and B are codominant to each other, and both are dominant over O. Genotypes: AA or AO = type A, BB or BO = type B, AB = type AB, OO = type O.

08Evolution & Natural Selection

Evolution is the change in allele frequencies in a population over time. It is the unifying theme of all biology. Natural selection is the primary mechanism of evolution.

How Natural Selection Works

Four conditions must be present: variation exists in the population, some of that variation is heritable (genetic), more offspring are produced than can survive (overproduction), and individuals with advantageous traits survive and reproduce more (differential reproduction). Over generations, favorable traits become more common.

Natural selection acts on phenotypes, not genotypes. Individuals do not evolve. Populations evolve. The environment determines which traits are advantageous.

Evidence for Evolution

EvidenceExplanation
Fossil recordShows change over time; transitional forms
Comparative anatomyHomologous structures (same structure, different function) indicate common ancestry
EmbryologySimilar early development across species
Molecular biologyDNA/protein similarity correlates with relatedness
BiogeographySpecies distribution matches evolutionary predictions

Other Key Concepts

09Ecology & the Environment

Ecology is the study of how organisms interact with each other and their environment. It spans from individual organisms to the entire biosphere.

Levels of Organization

Organism → population (same species, same area) → community (all species in an area) → ecosystem (community + physical environment) → biome (large region with similar climate/organisms) → biosphere (all life on Earth).

Energy Flow

Energy enters ecosystems as sunlight and flows one direction: producers → primary consumers → secondary consumers → tertiary consumers. Only about 10% of energy transfers between trophic levels; 90% is lost as heat. This is why food chains are typically short (4-5 levels).

Population Growth & Carrying Capacity

Exponential growth (J-curve) occurs with unlimited resources. Logistic growth (S-curve) levels off at carrying capacity (K), the maximum population an environment can sustain. Density-dependent factors (competition, disease, predation) intensify as population grows. Density-independent factors (fire, flood, drought) affect populations regardless of size.

Human Impact

Major environmental concerns include habitat destruction (the leading cause of species loss), climate change (rising CO₂ from burning fossil fuels traps heat), pollution, overexploitation of resources, and invasive species. Biodiversity is critical for ecosystem stability and resilience.

10Human Body Systems Overview

The human body is organized into organ systems that work together to maintain homeostasis, the stable internal environment necessary for survival.

IntegumentarySkin, hair, nailsProtection, temperature regulation, sensation
SkeletalBones, cartilage, jointsSupport, protection, movement, blood cell production
MuscularSkeletal, smooth, cardiac muscleMovement, posture, heat generation
NervousBrain, spinal cord, nervesRapid signaling, sensation, coordination, thought
EndocrineGlands (pituitary, thyroid, adrenals, pancreas)Hormone production for slow, long-lasting regulation
CardiovascularHeart, blood vessels, bloodTransports O₂, nutrients, waste, hormones
RespiratoryLungs, trachea, diaphragmGas exchange: O₂ in, CO₂ out
DigestiveMouth, stomach, intestines, liver, pancreasBreak down food, absorb nutrients
UrinaryKidneys, bladder, uretersFilter blood, remove waste, regulate water/salt

ReproductiveOvaries/testes, uterusProduce gametes, support offspring development
Homeostasis is maintained through negative feedback loops. A sensor detects a change, a control center processes the information, and an effector responds to restore the set point. Example: body temperature rises → brain signals sweat glands → sweating cools the body back down.

Immune System Basics

Innate immunity is nonspecific (skin, mucus, inflammation, fever). Adaptive immunity is specific and involves lymphocytes: B cells produce antibodies, and T cells destroy infected cells. Vaccines expose the immune system to a harmless form of a pathogen so it can build memory cells for faster future responses.
